Every atom of calcium in your bones was forged inside a star that no longer exists. Every atom of iron in your blood was created in the final moments of a stellar death so violent it briefly outshone entire galaxies. We are not just connected to the cosmos in some poetic sense. We are literally built from it.

That process of creation and redistribution has a name: a supernova.

## What Triggers a Supernova

Not all stars end this way. Our Sun will not go supernova. It is too small. When it runs out of fuel in about five billion years, it will swell into a red giant, shed its outer layers as a glowing cloud of gas called a planetary nebula, and leave behind a dense white dwarf. Dramatic, but not catastrophic.

Supernovae require stars with much more mass, typically at least eight times the mass of the Sun. These are the stars that live fast and die spectacularly.

As described in the black hole post, massive stars fuse progressively heavier elements in nested shells, like an onion. Hydrogen burns in the outer layers. Helium, carbon, neon, oxygen, and silicon burn in successive inner shells. At the centre, iron accumulates.

Iron is the problem. Fusing iron does not release energy. It absorbs it. Once an iron core builds up to about 1.4 times the mass of the Sun, there is nothing left to stop gravity. The core collapses in roughly a quarter of a second, reaching densities comparable to an atomic nucleus. The infalling outer layers slam into this newly rigid core and bounce outward in a shockwave. That shockwave, energised further by a flood of neutrinos pouring out of the collapsing core, tears the star apart.

## What We See From Earth

From our perspective, a supernova appears as a new star blazing in the sky. The word itself comes from astronomers who initially thought they were observing a new star being born, rather than an old one dying.

At peak brightness, a supernova can outshine its entire host galaxy. The last supernova visible to the naked eye in our cosmic neighbourhood was **SN 1987A**, which appeared in February 1987 in the Large Magellanic Cloud, a satellite galaxy of the Milky Way about 168,000 light years away. Astronomers detected a pulse of neutrinos arriving at detectors on Earth several hours before the light. Those neutrinos had streamed straight through the collapsing star while the light had to fight its way out. It was the first time we had detected neutrinos from outside our solar system, and it confirmed decades of theoretical work.

## The Remnants

A supernova does not leave nothing behind. It leaves two things.

First, a rapidly expanding shell of gas called a **supernova remnant**. These nebulae glow for thousands of years as they expand and cool, seeding the interstellar medium with heavy elements. The Crab Nebula, visible in the constellation Taurus, is the remnant of a supernova observed by Chinese astronomers in 1054 AD. It is still expanding at roughly 1,500 kilometres per second.

Second, depending on the mass of the original star, the collapsed core becomes either a **neutron star** or a **black hole**. Neutron stars are extraordinary objects in their own right: city-sized spheres of nuclear-density matter, sometimes spinning hundreds of times per second, sweeping beams of radio waves across the sky like cosmic lighthouses. We call those pulsars.

## Why This Matters Beyond the Physics

The elements forged in stellar interiors and scattered by supernovae include most of the periodic table. Hydrogen and helium came from the Big Bang. Everything heavier, from carbon and oxygen to gold and uranium, was built by stars and distributed by their deaths.

The supernova remnants that drifted through the early solar system eventually accumulated into the Sun, the planets, and everything on them, including us. When a massive star dies, it is not simply ending. It is investing in everything that comes after.

There is something quietly astonishing about that. The violence of a stellar death is also an act of extraordinary generosity toward a universe that had not yet made us.
