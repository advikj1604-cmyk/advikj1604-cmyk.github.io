Imagine taking the Sun, roughly 1.4 million kilometres across, and compressing it into a sphere about 20 kilometres in diameter. The density would be so extreme that a single teaspoon of the material would weigh around a billion tonnes on Earth. Protons and electrons would be crushed together into neutrons. The entire object would be, in effect, a single atomic nucleus the size of a city.

That is a neutron star.

## Born From Catastrophe

Neutron stars are what remain when a massive star dies in a supernova, provided the collapsing core is not quite massive enough to form a black hole. The boundary is somewhere around three solar masses for the remnant core. Below that, neutron pressure can resist further collapse. Above it, nothing can, and a black hole forms instead.

The collapse from a stellar core to a neutron star takes less than a second. The density achieved is roughly equivalent to that of an atomic nucleus, but stretched across an object 20 kilometres wide. It is genuinely difficult to find analogies that do justice to how extreme this is.

## Pulsars

Shortly after neutron stars were theorised in the 1930s, they were largely considered mathematical curiosities. Then in 1967, a PhD student named **Jocelyn Bell Burnell** noticed a strange, perfectly regular radio signal in data from a new radio telescope in Cambridge. The pulses arrived every 1.3 seconds without variation.

The signal was briefly nicknamed LGM-1, for "Little Green Men." It seemed too regular to be natural. But within months, the explanation emerged. The source was a rapidly rotating neutron star with a powerful magnetic field, sweeping a beam of radio emission across the sky like a lighthouse. Every time the beam pointed toward Earth, a pulse was detected.

These are pulsars. Some rotate hundreds of times per second. The fastest known, PSR J1748-2446ad, spins at 716 times per second. Its equator moves at roughly 24 percent the speed of light. Despite this, pulsars are extraordinarily stable timekeepers. Some are more accurate than atomic clocks.

## What Is Inside

The interior of a neutron star is largely unknown. The outer crust is relatively well understood: a lattice of neutron-rich nuclei. Deeper in, neutrons likely form a fluid, possibly a superfluid. At the very centre, pressures are so extreme that matter may exist in states we cannot reproduce in any laboratory.

## Gravitational Waves From a Collision

In August 2017, two neutron stars 130 million light years away spiralled together and merged. The event, designated **GW170817**, was detected by the LIGO and Virgo gravitational wave observatories as a ripple in spacetime. Within two seconds, a burst of gamma rays arrived. Then, over the following days and weeks, telescopes around the world watched the aftermath glow across every wavelength of light.

That glow was a kilonova: the radioactive decay of heavy elements freshly forged in the collision. Gold. Platinum. Uranium. Your gold jewellery was almost certainly forged in a collision like this, billions of years ago, before the solar system existed.

## Magnetars

Some neutron stars develop magnetic fields trillions of times stronger than Earth's. These are called magnetars. On December 27, 2004, a magnetar in our galaxy released a burst of energy in about a tenth of a second that briefly ionised the upper atmosphere of Earth from 50,000 light years away. It was the most powerful event ever detected from outside our solar system, and it was a relatively minor episode for the magnetar itself.

The universe is dramatic in ways that are easy to underestimate from a clear night in your backyard.
