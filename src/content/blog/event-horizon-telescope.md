On April 10, 2019, a press conference was held simultaneously in six cities around the world. At each one, researchers unveiled the same image: a blurry orange ring surrounding a dark centre. It looked almost underwhelming until you understood what you were looking at.

That ring was light bending around a black hole 55 million light years away, at the centre of a galaxy called M87. The dark centre was the shadow of the event horizon itself. It was the first time anyone had directly seen one.

## Why It Took This Long

The challenge with imaging a black hole is one of scale. Even the largest black holes, when viewed from Earth, appear extraordinarily small in the sky. M87's central black hole is about 6.5 billion times the mass of the Sun and physically enormous, but at 55 million light years away, its apparent size in the sky is roughly the same as an orange sitting on the surface of the Moon as seen from Earth.

No single telescope has anywhere near the resolution needed to image something that small. But there is a trick: the resolution of a radio telescope is determined not just by its dish size, but by the distance between dishes used together as an array. Link telescopes across the entire planet, and you effectively create a telescope the size of Earth.

That is exactly what the Event Horizon Telescope is. Not a single instrument but a global network of radio observatories from Antarctica to Hawaii to Spain to Chile, all pointing at the same target at the same time and recording data on specialised hard drives that were later physically shipped to a central location for processing.

## The Data Problem

Each telescope recorded enormous amounts of data. The total dataset from the 2017 observation run (the images were released in 2019 after processing) was around five petabytes. That is five million gigabytes. The internet connections at some of the sites were too slow to transmit this volume, so the drives from the South Pole Telescope literally had to wait until winter ended and a plane could land.

The data from each telescope then needed to be combined, a process called very long baseline interferometry. The timing had to be precise to within a fraction of a nanosecond. Atomic clocks at each site kept everything synchronised.

## What the Image Shows

The bright ring in the image is not the surface of the black hole. It is superheated plasma in the accretion disk, the swirling material being drawn toward the event horizon, glowing at millions of degrees. The light from the far side of the disk is bent around the black hole by its gravity and arrives at our telescopes from directions you would not naively expect.

The dark region in the centre is sometimes called the "shadow." It is not quite the event horizon itself but rather the region of sky from which no light can reach us, bounded by what is called the photon sphere, where light can orbit the black hole. The actual event horizon is somewhat smaller, hidden within the shadow.

One side of the ring appears brighter than the other. This is because the plasma on one side is moving toward us, which makes its emission appear brighter due to a relativistic effect called Doppler beaming.

## Sagittarius A

In 2022, the same collaboration released a second image: this time of **Sagittarius A***, the black hole at the centre of our own Milky Way. It is far closer, about 27,000 light years away, but it is also far smaller, about four million solar masses compared to M87's 6.5 billion.

That smaller mass creates a problem. The plasma near Sagittarius A* orbits in minutes rather than days, meaning the target was visibly changing during the observations. It was like trying to photograph a child who refuses to hold still, while M87 was more like photographing a mountain. The team had to develop new imaging techniques to produce a stable picture.

The result, when it arrived, looked remarkably similar to the M87 image. A glowing ring. A dark centre. General relativity, once again, describing reality with uncomfortable precision.

## What Comes Next

The Event Horizon Telescope collaboration is working on expanding the array, adding more telescopes and moving to shorter wavelengths to improve resolution. The eventual goal is a movie rather than a photograph: enough temporal resolution to watch plasma actually flowing around the event horizon in real time.

We went from black holes being theoretical curiosities to having photographs of two of them within a single lifetime. That trajectory suggests the next decade of observations will be something to watch.
