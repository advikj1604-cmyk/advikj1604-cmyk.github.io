When astronomers look at a galaxy and measure how fast the stars at its edges are orbiting the centre, something is wrong. Based on the visible mass, those outer stars should be moving slowly, just as Pluto moves more slowly around the Sun than Mercury does. But they are not. They orbit at roughly the same speed as stars much closer to the galactic centre.

Either our understanding of gravity is wrong, or there is a vast amount of matter out there that we simply cannot see. Decades of evidence now points strongly toward the second option. We call it dark matter.

## The Evidence

The rotation curve problem is just one of several independent lines of evidence. Gravitational lensing shows that clusters of galaxies bend light by far more than their visible mass could account for. The cosmic microwave background, the faint afterglow of the Big Bang, encodes information about the relative amounts of ordinary and dark matter in the early universe. Computer simulations of how galaxies and large-scale structure form only match observations when dark matter is included.

None of these observations involve the same physical effect. They are all pointing at the same conclusion from different directions. That is a strong indicator that dark matter is real.

## What It Is Not

Dark matter is not ordinary matter that is simply dark, like dust, dead stars, or black holes. There are not enough of these objects to account for the missing mass, and they would leave other observable signatures that are not seen.

It is not antimatter. Antimatter annihilates with ordinary matter and produces gamma rays. We do not see the signature of this happening in the amounts that would be required.

It is almost certainly not a modification to gravity, though modified gravity theories remain a minority view. The most difficult observation for these theories to explain is the **Bullet Cluster**: two galaxy clusters that collided, with the hot gas (the ordinary matter) slowing down due to electromagnetic interactions while the gravitational mass of each cluster passed straight through. The mass, as measured by lensing, separated from the visible matter. That behaviour is exactly what you would expect from dark matter particles that barely interact with anything.

## What It Might Be

The leading candidate for decades has been **WIMPs**, Weakly Interacting Massive Particles. These would be particles with masses somewhat similar to an atomic nucleus but interacting only through gravity and the weak nuclear force. Many experiments have searched for WIMPs. None have definitively found them.

Other candidates include axions, sterile neutrinos, primordial black holes (formed before stars existed, and therefore not made of ordinary matter), and a wide range of more exotic proposals.

We genuinely do not know what dark matter is. We know how much there is (roughly five times more dark matter than ordinary matter by mass), we know how it behaves gravitationally, and we know what it is not. What it actually is remains one of the most significant open questions in physics.

## The Larger Picture

When you look up at a clear sky, you are seeing the minority. All the stars, gas, planets, and dust that make up the visible universe account for less than five percent of the total energy content of the cosmos. Dark matter is about 27 percent. The remaining 68 percent is something even stranger: dark energy, the name we give to the unknown cause of the universe's accelerating expansion.

In other words, everything we have ever directly observed is a thin layer of ordinary matter floating in a vast ocean of things we do not yet understand. That is either deeply unsettling or extraordinarily exciting, depending on how you look at it.

I find it exciting. It means the most interesting discoveries are almost certainly still ahead.
